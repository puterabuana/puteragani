The Mythology of Time — Time, Power & the Modern World

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 9, 2026 3 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Industrial time

We have seen how profoundly time-cultures differ, and how deeply language and neuroscience encode those differences in the mind. But there is a third chapter to this story — perhaps the most consequential one — which is about power. About what happens when one time-culture, backed by industrial and military force, imposes itself on all the others. And about what the modern world, perpetually accelerating, may be doing to the very capacity for varied temporal experience.

The Conquest of Global Time

The standardization of global time is one of the least-examined dimensions of colonialism, yet one of the most pervasive. Consider what happened, systematically, across the 18th, 19th, and 20th centuries: indigenous peoples who organized their lives around seasonal, cyclical, and cosmological time-systems were required to organize themselves according to the industrial clock — for the factory, the plantation, the colonial administration, the railroad schedule.

1847
British Railway Time

Britain adopts a single standardized "railway time" to synchronize train schedules — the first time an entire nation's diverse local times were unified under one clock. Local solar time, which varied up to 30 minutes across the country, became legally irrelevant.

1884
The Prime Meridian Conference

Twenty-five nations meet in Washington D.C. and agree to divide the earth into 24 time zones radiating from Greenwich, England. The rest of the world's time is now defined in relation to a meridian chosen for British imperial convenience.

1900s
Colonial Time Imposition

Colonial administrators across Africa, Asia, and the Americas systematically dismantled indigenous calendars, seasonal rituals, and time-keeping systems — often deliberately, as part of the project of cultural assimilation and labor discipline.

1972
Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)

The world adopts atomic-clock-based UTC. Time is now measured to nanosecond precision. The global financial system, internet, and logistics networks are built on this absolute temporal grid — making all other time-orientations, in practice, subordinate to it.

This is not merely historical. The global economy runs on a single temporal logic — the monochronic, productivity-oriented, present-to-future linear time of industrial capitalism. Organizations in polychronic cultures that do business internationally are often forced to adopt a parallel time-culture for external interactions while maintaining a different one internally. The cognitive and organizational tax of this dual-time existence is real, if largely unmeasured.

The Acceleration Problem

When Time Itself Speeds Up

Beyond the homogenization of global time-cultures, the modern world confronts a second temporal crisis: acceleration. Sociologist Hartmut Rosa has argued that late modernity is defined not merely by change but by the structural acceleration of the pace of change itself — technological acceleration, social acceleration, and the acceleration of the pace of life feeding back on each other in a self-reinforcing loop.

The consequences are not trivial. When the pace of change exceeds the pace at which cultural institutions, ethical frameworks, and individual psychologies can adapt, the result is what Rosa calls "social desynchronization" — a loss of the shared temporal horizons that allow communities to coordinate meaning, plan collectively, and maintain continuity between generations. Cultures that processed time in slower, cyclical rhythms had different but not necessarily inferior tools for human flourishing. Many of those tools are disappearing under the pressure of a single accelerating global tempo.

"The question is not whether we can stop time's acceleration. It is whether we can choose, deliberately, which temporal rhythms to protect — and which to resist."

What Is Worth Recovering

The conclusion of this inquiry is not nostalgia. You cannot and should not wish yourself back into a pre-modern relationship with time. The coordination benefits of global standardized time are real. The medical advances that require precision timing are real. The efficiency gains of monochronic productivity are real, in limited domains.

But taking seriously the diversity of time-cultures suggests that we have traded away more than we acknowledge. Specifically, three things seem worth deliberate recovery:

🔄
Cyclical Rhythms

Sabbaths, feast cycles, seasonal pauses — built-in temporal interruptions that prevent the indefinite acceleration of the present moment and restore perspective.

🌱
Long Horizons

The Confucian and indigenous traditions of planning across generations rather than quarters. What would it mean to design institutions for 200-year timescales?

🎯
Present Depth

The Buddhist and contemplative insistence that the present moment, fully inhabited, contains more than any amount of future-chasing. Attention itself is a temporal practice.

The Personal Question

At the individual level, the mythology of time you live inside is not simply given. It is, to a surprising degree, a choice — or at least something that can be examined, questioned, and partially reshaped. The most productive question to ask yourself is not "am I managing my time well?" — that question already accepts, uncritically, the industrial capitalist premise that time is a resource to be managed. A more interesting question is: what temporal mythology am I actually living inside, and is it mine?

✦ Final Thought

Every culture has handed down a story about what time is — linear or cyclical, scarce or abundant, arrow or ocean. You have inherited one such story, probably without knowing it. The point of this inquiry is not to replace your story with a better one. It is to make the story visible — and in doing so, to recover what every tradition, in its own way, has always known: how you hold time is how you hold your life.
