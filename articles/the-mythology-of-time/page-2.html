The Mythology of Time — Language, Neuroscience & the Brain

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 9, 2026 4 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Neuroscience concept

If Page 1 established that cultures differ dramatically in their relationship to time, a more demanding question follows: is this purely a social convention, or does the language you grow up speaking actually rewire how your brain experiences time? The answer, according to a growing body of cognitive science research, is disquieting in its implications — and more interesting than most popular accounts acknowledge.

When Language Shapes Perception

The debate about whether language shapes thought — known in linguistics as the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, or linguistic relativity — has seesawed for a century. The strong version of the claim (that language determines what you can think) is now largely discredited. But the weak version — that language influences the ease, speed, and default orientation of certain kinds of thought — has accumulated striking experimental support.

Time is one of the clearest test cases. Speakers of Mandarin Chinese commonly use vertical spatial metaphors for time — earlier events are "up," later events are "down" — whereas English speakers predominantly use horizontal metaphors (the future is "ahead," the past is "behind"). When psychologists tested whether this linguistic difference produced actual cognitive differences, Mandarin speakers were measurably faster at confirming temporal relationships when primed with vertical spatial cues. The metaphor was not just decorative; it shaped the mental machinery of temporal reasoning.

The Aymara Case

The case that most challenges Western assumptions comes from the Aymara people of the Andean highlands. In Aymara, the past is in front of you, and the future is behind. The logic is, once you understand it, impeccable: you can see the past, you have evidence of it — it is visible, known, in front of your eyes. The future is unknown, unseeable — it is behind you, at your back.

Researchers found that Aymara speakers accompany their speech with gestures that confirm this orientation: when talking about past events, they gesture forward; for future events, they gesture backward. This is not merely a linguistic quirk. It reflects a fundamentally different relationship between epistemology and temporality — what you know versus what you can only guess.

Language / Culture Time Direction Key Metaphor
English Horizontal (forward = future) "Looking forward to tomorrow"
Mandarin Vertical (up = earlier) "Last week" = "上个星期" (upper week)
Aymara (Andes) Reversed horizontal (front = past) Past is visible, future is behind you
Kuuk Thaayorre (Australia) Cardinal / absolute (East to West) Time flows with the sun, independent of body position

"If you asked me what time feels like, I would say: it depends entirely on the grammar in which I first learned to count the hours."

— Rafael Núñez, cognitive scientist, University of California San Diego

The Neuroscience of Cultural Time

Beyond language, neuroscientists have identified several ways that cultural time-orientation appears to produce measurable differences in brain function and behavior. These findings come from a combination of fMRI studies, behavioral economics experiments, and cross-cultural psychology research conducted over the past two decades.

🔬 Key Research Findings — Cross-Cultural Neuroscience of Time
⏳

Future Discounting Rates

Economic studies across 45 countries found that future-oriented cultures (those with strong grammar-based future tense distinctions) show significantly lower rates of hyperbolic discounting — they are more willing to wait for larger rewards rather than taking smaller immediate ones. The future, grammatically close, is psychologically closer too.

🧠

Hippocampal Encoding of Episodes

Research at the intersection of anthropology and neuroscience suggests that cultures with cyclical time concepts show different patterns of autobiographical memory consolidation — events are encoded more as instances of recurring types rather than unique linear episodes, affecting which details are prioritized for retention.

🎯

Attention and Present-Focus

Meditation traditions embedded in Buddhist cyclical time-cultures have produced measurable neurological effects on attention and present-moment awareness that are now well-documented in contemplative neuroscience. Whether this is cause or effect of the time-culture is still debated — but the correlation is robust.

📅

Prospective Memory

Cultures with dense future-planning rituals (calendar-obsessed, deadline-driven societies) show stronger performance on laboratory tests of prospective memory — the ability to remember to do something at a future point. The cognitive muscle appears to strengthen with cultural use.

The Tension Between Inner and Social Time

One of the most striking insights from research into time perception is that there are actually multiple time systems operating simultaneously within every person — and cultural pressure can force them into conflict. The body runs on biological time: circadian rhythms, ultradian cycles, the hormonal ebb and flow of sleep and wakefulness. These are ancient, universal, and stubbornly non-negotiable. Social time — the schedules, deadlines, and calendars imposed by culture and economy — often runs at a very different rhythm.

Biological Time

Circadian rhythms, sleep cycles, hunger cues. Varies by individual chronotype. Cannot be fully overridden. Demanding we wake at 6 AM despite a 2 AM sleep time is a form of time-culture violence against the body.

Social Time

Calendars, deadlines, work hours, meal schedules. Culturally constructed. Varies enormously. Some cultures (Spain, parts of Latin America) have social time-structures that align far better with Mediterranean biological rhythms.

The tension between these systems is not minor. Research on social jetlag — the chronic misalignment between biological and social time — suggests it is as physiologically damaging as shift work, contributing to metabolic disruption, impaired cognition, and mood dysregulation. Cultures that have preserved social rhythms more in tune with biological ones may, paradoxically, carry a health advantage their GDP statistics don't capture.
