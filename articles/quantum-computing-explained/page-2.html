Quantum Computing Explained: The Industry Race

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 9, 2026 10 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Quantum computing race

In the past decade, the race to build a practical quantum computer has become one of the most competitive frontiers in technology. Billions of dollars are being poured into quantum research. Every major tech company — and many startups — are fighting for advantage. This is not purely scientific anymore. It is geopolitical. It is about economic dominance.

The Major Players

IBM has been investing in quantum computing for nearly two decades. They operate their quantum computers as a public service through IBM Quantum, offering access to researchers and developers worldwide. Their latest processors have around 400-500 qubits. IBM's strategy is to make quantum accessible — betting that by democratizing access, they position themselves as the trusted infrastructure provider for the quantum era.

Google announced "quantum supremacy" in 2019 — claiming their Sycamore processor solved a problem in 200 seconds that would take a classical supercomputer 10,000 years. The announcement sparked controversy about whether the problem was practically meaningful. Google is betting that breakthroughs in error correction and qubit stability will eventually unlock massive commercial value.

IonQ and other startups are pursuing alternative approaches using trapped ions, photonic systems, or quantum annealers. Rather than compete with Google and IBM on pure scale, they are betting on novel approaches that might leapfrog established techniques — finding a niche, then dominating it.

"The first company to achieve practical quantum advantage will have extraordinary market advantage — but we don't yet know which approach will win."

The competition is fierce. Quantum computing is seen as a strategic technology that could reshape entire industries. The stakes are enormous. National governments are funding quantum research heavily, viewing it as crucial to future competitiveness and national security.

The Real Obstacles

Despite all the investment, quantum computing still faces fundamental challenges. Error correction is the biggest. Qubits are fragile, and quantum computers need error correction codes that use many physical qubits to create fewer logical qubits. Estimates suggest one logical qubit might require 1000+ physical qubits — we're nowhere near that yet.

Scalability is another fundamental barrier. Building 50 qubits is hard. Building 500 is harder. Building 1 million is exponentially more difficult. No clear path exists yet from current systems to the scale needed for commercial applications. And decoherence — the loss of quantum properties in microseconds — remains the primary technical constraint on any practical calculation.

Where Quantum Impact Might Come First

Despite the obstacles, certain applications are within reach in the next 5-10 years. Pharmaceutical companies are actively preparing quantum strategies. Simulating molecular interactions faster could accelerate drug discovery by years, saving billions in R&D. Quantum computers could redesign materials, accelerate financial modeling, and break current encryption systems.

But here is the honest truth: no quantum computer has solved a commercially valuable problem faster than a classical computer. That is the honest truth beneath all the marketing. Quantum advantage remains theoretical or narrowly demonstrable in labs. The question is: when — if ever — will that change?

"Quantum computing will not replace classical computers. It will specialize. Success means finding the narrow problems where quantum excels."

The Investment Bubble Risk

There is a real risk that quantum computing becomes an investment bubble — a space where capital flows based on hype and theoretical potential rather than demonstrated value. Some venture capital firms are betting on quantum startups despite fundamental physics remaining unsolved. Government funding is enormous, but much of it targets moonshot technology rather than near-term applications.
