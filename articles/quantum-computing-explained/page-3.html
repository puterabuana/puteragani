Quantum Computing Explained: The Future

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 9, 2026 3 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Quantum future

So far we have explored the physics and the race. But we have not answered the deeper question: Will quantum computing ultimately transform civilization, or will it remain a specialized tool? Will it be revolutionary or evolutionary?

History provides clues. When computers first emerged in the 1950s, experts predicted they would remain rare, expensive machines used only by governments and large corporations. Personal computers and the internet changed that. Will quantum computing follow a similar trajectory, or is it fundamentally different?

Three Possible Futures

Scenario 1: The Quantum Revolution. By 2035-2040, engineers overcome decoherence and error correction. Quantum computers become practical. They accelerate drug discovery, materials science, and optimization across industries. New companies are built on quantum advantage. Governments compete for quantum supremacy. (Likelihood: 25-30%)

Scenario 2: The Specialized Tool. Quantum computers remain difficult to build. They find specific niches: pharmaceutical research, cryptanalysis, materials research. Cloud access becomes standard, but impact is limited. Most computation remains classical. (Likelihood: 45-50%)

Scenario 3: The False Promise. After decades and billions invested, fundamental physics barriers prove insurmountable at scale. Quantum computers never achieve practical advantage. Hype gives way to disappointment. (Likelihood: 20-25%)

"If forced to bet, the specialized tool scenario is most likely. Quantum will work, but won't be a replacement."

The honest assessment: quantum computers will probably work. They will probably solve specific, important problems faster than classical computers. But they will probably never be as general-purpose. They are not a replacement — they are a specialist complement.

The Security Imperative

One quantum outcome is already certain: the cryptographic disruption. If large-scale quantum computers ever become real, they will break RSA encryption — the foundation of modern internet security. That threat is serious enough that governments are already preparing.

The U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has been working on "post-quantum cryptography" — encryption algorithms secure even against quantum attacks. These are being standardized now for deployment in coming years. Power grids, water systems, nuclear plants, and financial networks are transitioning to quantum-resistant encryption. The "harvest now, decrypt later" threat is real: adversaries are already recording encrypted communications, planning to decrypt them when quantum computers arrive.

What You Should Do Now

If you are an individual: Most of this is beyond your control. But be aware that encryption protecting your data today might not protect it forever. Choose companies that take security seriously.

If you are an organization: Audit your cryptographic infrastructure now. Develop a transition plan to post-quantum cryptography. This is not optional — it is infrastructure maintenance.

If you are an investor or entrepreneur: Quantum is a real frontier with real potential. But separate hype from reality. Winners will solve specific, narrow problems — not general-purpose quantum computers. Look for applications in drug discovery, materials science, and optimization.

If you are a researcher: We need you. The fundamental challenges in quantum error correction, decoherence, and scalability remain unsolved. This is open territory with genuine scientific problems and enormous stakes.
