Web3's Future: Where Blockchain Might Actually Belong

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 09, 2026 6 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Technology Future

If blockchain is neither the revolutionary solution some promised nor the complete fraud others claim, what is it? Where does it actually belong? After the hype subsides and the scammers are prosecuted, what remains?

Payments in Broken Money Systems

The strongest case for cryptocurrency remains the original one: a money substitute for people in countries where the official money has failed. When your government's currency is experiencing hyperinflation, or when your country is under economic sanctions, or when your banking system is controlled by a corrupt regime, bitcoin offers a real alternative.

This is not a theoretical argument. In El Salvador, crypto adoption has been driven partly by practical necessity. In Venezuela, where the bolivar has been destroyed by hyperinflation, bitcoin and stablecoins have become shadow currency. Transactions that would be impossible through official banking channels can happen on blockchain. The speed is slow, the volatility is extreme, but it beats the alternative of having no functional money at all.

This use case doesn't require revolution. It just requires that crypto exist as an option when formal systems fail.

✅ Blockchain Use Cases That Might Persist
01

Cross-Border Remittances

Sending money internationally without banks. Stablecoins offer cheaper, faster transfers than Western Union. The friction in traditional systems creates genuine opportunity for blockchain here.

02

Settlement Systems

Financial institutions might use blockchain for back-office settlement between banks, replacing legacy systems that are slow and expensive. This is less sexy than "decentralizing finance" but potentially valuable.

03

Supply Chain Verification

Creating immutable records of provenance for luxury goods, pharmaceuticals, or agricultural products. Not because blockchain solves anything better than databases, but because immutability provides auditable proof.

04

Digital Contracts in Contested Domains

Situations where neither party trusts a central authority. Escrow, microtransactions, or agreements that need cryptographic verification without intermediaries.

The future of blockchain is smaller, more specific, and vastly less revolutionary than the rhetoric suggests.

The Sobering Reality

Cryptocurrency has been trying to replace or improve upon every financial system for 15+ years. It has succeeded in very few domains. Bitcoin remains volatile. Ethereum remains a playground for speculation. Stablecoins exist to prop up crypto exchanges. Layer-2 solutions exist because the base layers don't work for actual use. The more successful blockchain becomes at solving a specific problem, the more it gets regulated into a hybrid system that reintroduces intermediaries.

This isn't failure. It's maturation. Every transformative technology promises to revolutionize everything, then settles into specific domains where it's actually valuable. The internal combustion engine was going to eliminate horses — and it did, for transportation. But horses still exist for other purposes. Blockchain isn't going to decentralize everything. It might do one or two things better than alternatives. That's okay.

🔮 Likely Future

Blockchain becomes a boring utility technology. A few cryptocurrencies survive as money substitutes in broken systems. Some financial institutions use blockchain for settlement. Smart contracts find niches. The technology exists, but it's not revolutionary — it's just another tool with specific applications and specific limitations.

What This Means for You

If you're building a product or service, the default assumption should be: you don't need blockchain. Databases work fine. Centralized payment processors work fine. Smart contracts introduce more security surface area than they solve in most domains. The burden of proof should be on blockchain proponents to explain why decentralization is necessary for your specific problem, not the other way around.

If you're investing, remember that most crypto will go to zero. The survivors will be boring. Bitcoin might persist as a store-of-value alternative. Some stablecoins might become infrastructure. But the projects promising to revolutionize finance, replace databases, or create digital utopias? Those are likely to be valuable primarily as speculative assets, not as functional technology.

The hardest lesson of Web3 is not that blockchain is bad. It's that it's not magical. It solves specific problems in specific domains by making different trade-offs than alternatives. Sometimes those trade-offs are worth it. Usually, they're not. Maturity means understanding the difference.

💭 The Bottom Line

Web3 isn't a revolution. It's a technology looking for problems. Some of those problems will be real. Most will be manufactured to justify the technology's existence. The intelligent path forward isn't religious enthusiasm or reflexive dismissal — it's rigorous skepticism applied to each claim. Ask: does this problem actually need decentralization? Would blockchain genuinely make it better? If the answer is no, you don't need it. That clarity is rarer and more valuable than any blockchain.
