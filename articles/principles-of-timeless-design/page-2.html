Timeless Design — Honesty, Restraint, and the Long Game

P
Putera Buana Gani
April 28, 2025 2 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Minimal workspace

Continuing through Rams' principles, we arrive at the qualities that most sharply distinguish genuine design from its imitations: honesty, restraint, environmental consideration, and the long view. These are not aesthetic preferences — they are ethical commitments about what design is ultimately for and what it owes to the people who encounter it.

Good Design Is Honest

The fifth principle strikes at something close to a moral claim. An honest design neither appears more innovative than it is, nor more valuable, nor more powerful. It does not make promises through its appearance that its function cannot keep. A product that looks precision-engineered from machined aluminum but is in fact hollow plastic is dishonest — not merely cheap, but a form of deception.

In the digital realm, this principle maps onto the ethics of interface design. Dark patterns — design choices deliberately engineered to manipulate users into actions they would not otherwise take — are perhaps the most vivid contemporary example of dishonest design. The pre-checked subscription box. The consent interface that buries the "decline" option in six-point type. The cancellation flow that requires seven steps while signup required one. These are not design failures; they are design choices. Dishonest ones.

"Good design is honest. It does not make a product appear more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is. It does not attempt to manipulate the consumer and it does not deceive." — Dieter Rams

Good Design Is Unobtrusive

Products that fulfil a purpose, Rams argued, are like tools. They are neither decorative objects nor works of art. Their design should therefore be both neutral and restrained, leaving room for the user's self-expression. The sixth principle is a rebuke to the designer's ego — a reminder that the object is not a canvas for self-expression, but a service to the person who uses it.

This is the philosophical root of what we now call "invisible design" — the aspiration to create products so perfectly suited to their purpose that the design itself disappears. The ideal chair does not announce itself; it simply holds you comfortably while you forget it exists. The ideal text editor does not display its interface; it puts words between you and the page without friction.

Good Design Is Long-lasting

Unlike fashion, good design avoids being fashionable and therefore never appears antiquated. It is conceived to endure across time, accumulating meaning and patina rather than obsolescence. This is a principle that runs directly against the commercial logic of planned obsolescence and the churn of trend cycles — which is precisely why it is so rarely followed, and so easy to identify when it is.

Consider what it means in practice: a well-designed object should look as appropriate, as purposeful, as right in 30 years as it does today. The Braun SK 4 record player Rams designed in 1956 still looks current. The Eames Lounge Chair from the same era feels as precisely right in a contemporary room as it did in a mid-century one. These objects are not timeless because they are conservative — it's because every design decision was in service of function, not fashion.

Good Design Is Environmentally Friendly

Rams added this principle later, and it may be the most prescient. Design, he argued, makes an important contribution to the preservation of the environment. It conserves resources, minimizes physical and visual pollution throughout the lifecycle of a product. In 1978, this was forward-thinking. In 2025, it is urgent.

The environmental calculus of design has never been more consequential. Every material choice, every manufacturing decision, every design-for-disassembly consideration or its absence, contributes to a product's environmental cost across its entire lifecycle. Designers who ignore this are not being neutral — they are making a choice with real consequences for ecosystems and communities that will bear those costs long after the product has been discarded.

Good Design Is as Little Design as Possible

The tenth principle is the most quoted and perhaps the most misunderstood. "Less but better" does not mean minimalism as aesthetic — it is not about spare interiors and empty shelves. It means concentrating on what is essential, not burdening products with non-essentials, not sacrificing quality to produce quantity. It is a principle of focus, of discipline, of genuine commitment to the user's actual needs rather than the designer's or marketer's desire to add more.

The hardest thing in design — in almost any creative discipline — is knowing what to leave out. Addition is easy. Subtraction requires confidence, judgment, and a clear-eyed commitment to the essentials. It requires asking, of every element: what would be lost if this were removed? If the honest answer is "nothing," then it should be removed.
