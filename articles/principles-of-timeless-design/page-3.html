Timeless Design — Applying the Principles in the Digital Age

P
Putera Gani Editorial
April 28, 2025 2 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Design workspace

The principles Dieter Rams articulated for physical industrial products translate with surprising precision into the digital realm — sometimes with even greater urgency, given the scale at which digital products operate and the intimacy with which they are woven into daily life.

The Digital Interface as Industrial Product

A smartphone app is, in every meaningful sense, an industrial product. It is designed, manufactured (in the sense of being engineered and built), distributed, used daily, and eventually replaced. The same questions Rams asked of a record player or a calculator apply to it: Is it innovative in a way that genuinely serves its users? Is it honest about what it does? Is it unobtrusive, leaving room for the user's own purposes? Is it built to last — or designed to be discarded with the next version cycle?

Most digital products, evaluated against these criteria, fail significantly. They are cluttered with features added to justify updates rather than serve users. They obscure their true function — harvesting data and attention — beneath appealing surfaces. They are deliberately unrestrained, engineered to be maximally sticky rather than maximally useful. They are planned to obsolesce, not to endure.

The Brave Designers

Against this backdrop, the designers and teams who have applied something like Rams' principles to digital products stand out clearly. The original iPhone interface — spare, legible, focused — was a deliberate rejection of the feature-crowded interfaces that dominated mobile computing at the time. Notion's document editor prioritizes the writing itself over the interface. Basecamp's design philosophy has always emphasized calm software that respects users' attention and time.

"Most of our competitors are trying to add more features. We're trying to figure out what to remove."

These are not accidents. They are the product of design cultures that take seriously the idea that software has an obligation to its users — not merely to engage them, but to serve them honestly. They are harder to build, harder to monetize in the short term, and harder to sell to boards obsessed with engagement metrics. They are also, arguably, the products that earn the deepest loyalty and the most enduring relevance.

What Designers Can Do Today

For individual designers working within organizations that may not share this philosophy, the principles offer both a compass and a practical framework. Before every design decision — before adding a feature, a notification, a modal, a tooltip — the question should be: does this genuinely serve the person using this product? If the honest answer is no, or even not clearly yes, the decision should be revisited.

Advocacy matters too. Design decisions are never purely aesthetic — they are always also ethical and commercial. Designers who can articulate why a simpler, more honest, more restrained design serves the long-term interests of both users and the business are performing a different and more valuable function than those who simply execute specifications. The principles of good design are not an aesthetic taste. They are a value system. And in a world of increasingly powerful and intimate technologies, that value system matters more than ever.

Dieter Rams once said that indifference towards people and the reality in which they live is actually the one and only cardinal sin in design. Sixty years after he wrote his principles, and in an era of design decisions that affect billions of lives simultaneously, that indifference — and its alternative — have never mattered more.
