The Principles of Timeless Design That Never Go Out of Style

P
Putera Buana Gani
April 28, 2025 6 min read
Page 1 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Design

In 1978, a German industrial designer working for the consumer electronics company Braun articulated ten principles that he believed defined what good design truly meant. His name was Dieter Rams. His principles — spare, precise, and demanding — were largely ignored by mainstream commercial design at the time. Today, they form the philosophical bedrock of some of the most celebrated products and interfaces in the world, from Apple's industrial design to Muji's retail aesthetic to the resurgence of Bauhaus principles in digital product design.

The remarkable thing about Rams' principles is not that they have aged well — it's that they appear almost immune to aging. In a field where trends cascade through like weather systems, leaving obsolescence in their wake, these ten commandments have only grown more relevant with time. Why? What makes a design principle truly timeless, as opposed to merely fashionable?

Good Design Is Innovative

Rams' first principle is also his most easily misread. Innovation in design, he argued, is not novelty for its own sake. It is the marriage of new possibilities with genuine human needs. A design that looks unlike anything that came before it, but solves no real problem better than its predecessors, is not innovative — it is merely new. The distinction matters enormously.

The history of design is littered with objects that were visually radical and commercially disastrous, not because the market was too conservative to appreciate them, but because the design failed to actually improve on what it replaced. Innovation, properly understood, is progressive. Each generation of a well-designed object should incorporate new possibilities created by new materials, manufacturing techniques, or technologies — but always in service of a better solution to a real problem.

Good Design Makes a Product Useful

The second principle cuts straight to what design is ultimately for. A product — whether physical or digital, mass-produced or bespoke — is purchased to be used. Its design must serve that use. Anything that interferes with use, or that treats use as secondary to visual impact, is a failure of design.

"Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works." — Steve Jobs, whose appreciation of Rams was lifelong and well-documented

This seems obvious. And yet the design world continually produces objects whose visual complexity or formal ambition actively impedes their function. Menus that cannot be navigated. Chairs that cannot be sat in comfortably. Bottles that cannot be opened without instruction. When aesthetics and utility conflict in a finished design, the designer has failed at the most fundamental level — regardless of how beautiful the object might be in a museum vitrine.

Good Design Is Aesthetic

The third principle is more nuanced than it first appears, because Rams was not making a superficial claim about beauty. His argument was that the aesthetic quality of a product is integral to its usefulness. Objects that are used daily — that are handled, observed, and lived with — affect the people who use them in ways that go beyond mere function. Ugly environments and ugly objects have measurable negative effects on mood, attention, and wellbeing. Beautiful objects, by contrast, invite use, encourage care, and create genuine pleasure.

This is not a license for ornament or decoration. Rams was deeply suspicious of both. The aesthetic he prized was one of clarity, restraint, and honesty — beauty that arises from the perfect execution of a form exactly suited to its function. It is an exacting standard, and it is one that almost no product fully achieves.

Good Design Makes a Product Understandable

Perhaps the most underappreciated of Rams' principles is the fourth: a well-designed product communicates its own nature and function. You should be able to pick up a well-designed object and understand what it is, what it does, and how to use it — without consulting a manual or tutorial.

This is what designers call "affordance" — the visual and physical properties of an object that suggest how it should be used. A handle invites gripping. A button invites pressing. A knob invites turning. When these cues are designed with care and honesty, the product teaches the user how to use it through its form alone. When they are absent, or misleading, the user is lost — and the failure belongs to the designer, not the user.
