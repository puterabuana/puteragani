The Dark Matter Problem — What Comes Next

P
Putera Gani
May 13, 2026 10 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Cosmos

The decades-long failure to detect dark matter particles has done something unexpected: it has opened the field. Where once there was near-consensus around WIMPs, there is now a rich diversity of theoretical proposals, experimental strategies, and — crucially — a willingness to question whether the dark matter paradigm itself needs revision. Science at its most productive looks exactly like this.

When the Data Doesn't Fit: Alternative Theories

A minority of physicists have long argued that the dark matter problem might be solved not by adding new particles, but by modifying our theory of gravity. If Newton's and Einstein's laws of gravity are slightly wrong at very low accelerations — the regime relevant to galaxy rotation curves — then the anomalies might disappear without any invisible matter at all.

⚖️

MOND — Modified Newtonian Dynamics

Proposed by Mordehai Milgrom in 1983, MOND modifies Newton's second law at very low accelerations. It successfully predicts galaxy rotation curves from the distribution of visible matter alone — a remarkable achievement that dark matter models struggle to match with equal precision. However, MOND fails badly at the scale of galaxy clusters, cannot explain the Bullet Cluster observations, and has no satisfactory relativistic extension. Most physicists regard it as a useful empirical relation rather than a fundamental theory.

🌀

TeVeS and Relativistic MOND

Jacob Bekenstein's Tensor-Vector-Scalar gravity (TeVeS) was an attempt to give MOND a relativistic foundation compatible with general relativity. It partially succeeded but required fine-tuning and struggled with gravitational wave observations. The detection of gravitational waves from a neutron star merger in 2017 — which arrived simultaneously with light — placed severe constraints on many modified gravity theories, including TeVeS variants.

🔗

Emergent Gravity

Erik Verlinde's 2016 proposal suggests that gravity itself is not a fundamental force but an emergent phenomenon arising from the thermodynamics of quantum information. In his framework, the apparent effects of dark matter are a consequence of the way ordinary matter interacts with the quantum information content of spacetime. The theory makes testable predictions that differ from both standard dark matter and MOND, and several observational tests are underway.

🌑

Self-Interacting Dark Matter

Rather than abandoning dark matter, some physicists propose that dark matter particles interact with each other — not just through gravity. Self-interacting dark matter (SIDM) could explain certain small-scale structure anomalies that standard cold dark matter models struggle with, such as the "core-cusp problem" in dwarf galaxies. SIDM remains within the dark matter paradigm but adds a rich internal physics to the dark sector.

What Discovery Would Mean

The Implications of Finding It

A confirmed dark matter detection would be one of the most consequential scientific events in history — comparable to the discovery of the electron, the confirmation of the atomic nucleus, or the detection of gravitational waves. The implications would cascade across physics, cosmology, and philosophy.

Question

What would a WIMP detection tell us about the Standard Model of particle physics?

Impact

It would almost certainly confirm supersymmetry — the theoretical framework predicting a "superpartner" for every known particle. This would be the first physics beyond the Standard Model, opening an entirely new chapter in fundamental physics and potentially explaining other mysteries like the matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe.

Question

What would an axion detection mean for our understanding of the early universe?

Impact

Axions would solve two major problems simultaneously — dark matter and the strong CP problem. Their detection would confirm that the universe underwent a phase transition called the Peccei-Quinn symmetry breaking, providing a new window into the first microseconds after the Big Bang and potentially connecting dark matter to the physics of inflation.

Question

What if dark matter turns out to be something entirely unexpected — outside all current theoretical frameworks?

Impact

This is arguably the most exciting possibility. History suggests that the most transformative discoveries are the ones nobody predicted. Dark matter could be a portal to an entirely new sector of physics — a "dark sector" with its own forces, particles, and interactions — that has been invisible to us simply because it doesn't couple to electromagnetism.

The Future of the Search

The next decade will see a new generation of experiments come online, each pushing sensitivity to previously inaccessible regimes. The field is not stagnating — it is accelerating, driven by advances in detector technology, computing power, and theoretical creativity.

The Philosophical Weight of the Invisible

There is something philosophically vertiginous about dark matter. For all of human history, we have assumed that the universe is, in principle, knowable through observation. We look up, we see stars, and we build models of what we see. Dark matter breaks that assumption at the most fundamental level: the majority of the universe is, by definition, beyond direct observation.

This is not merely a technical limitation. It is a statement about the nature of reality. The universe contains vast structures — halos, filaments, sheets of dark matter spanning hundreds of millions of light-years — that have never been and may never be directly seen. We infer their existence from their gravitational effects on the things we can see, the way a blind person might infer the shape of a room from the echoes of their footsteps.

And yet this invisible scaffolding is not peripheral. It is the dominant component of the universe's mass. It shaped the formation of every galaxy, every star, every planet. Without it, we would not exist. The atoms in your body were forged in stars that formed in dark matter halos that condensed from quantum fluctuations in the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang. Dark matter is not a curiosity at the edge of physics. It is the foundation on which everything visible is built.

🌌 The Bottom Line

Dark matter is the universe's most important unsolved problem. It is not a gap in our knowledge waiting to be filled — it is a fundamental challenge to our understanding of what matter, gravity, and reality itself actually are. The search for it is one of the great intellectual adventures of our time. And the answer, when it comes, will change everything.
