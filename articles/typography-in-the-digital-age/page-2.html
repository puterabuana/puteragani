Typography in the Digital Age — Design Mistakes

P
Putera Buana Gani
May 9, 2026 3 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Typography mistakes

Knowing typography principles and executing them well are two different things. The gap between intention and result is where most designers stumble. Understanding common mistakes is the fastest path to better designs.

Too Many Typefaces

The cardinal sin of typography is mixing too many typefaces. A document with four or five different typefaces feels chaotic. Professional design usually uses one, maybe two typefaces maximum. One for headings (often serif or display), one for body text (usually sans-serif on screens, serif in print).

When designers see a tool with 500,000 fonts available, the temptation is to use them. To show variety. To be creative. What actually happens is visual noise that makes text harder to read and the overall design appear amateur.

"Constraint is not the enemy of creativity. It is the foundation of good design."

Ignoring Readability for Aesthetics

A typeface can be beautiful and unreadable. Thin geometric sans-serifs look modern until you put them on a website at body text size. Decorative script fonts are lovely for a logo and terrible for paragraphs. High-contrast type can be cutting-edge and also cause eye strain.

The mistake is treating the two as separate. Readability IS aesthetics. A readable typeface feels better, looks better, communicates better than one that is hard to read. The audience does not consciously notice readability, but they feel its absence.

Mistake Impact on Reading Severity
Too small (below 14px) Eye strain, slower reading, comprehension drop CRITICAL
Too large (above 20px) Awkward, reads like headlines, loses professionalism MODERATE
Line length too long (>75 chars) Eye tracking difficulty, higher error rates HIGH
Low contrast ratio Accessibility issue, fatigue, exclusion of readers CRITICAL
All caps body text Slower reading speed by ~10%, appears aggressive HIGH
No line height adjustment Text appears cramped, harder to track lines MODERATE

Not Testing on Real Devices

A typeface looks good on a 27-inch monitor at 100% zoom. The same typeface often looks terrible on a phone screen. Thin strokes disappear. Small text becomes illegible. Colors shift. Designers who only preview work on their desktop make decisions that fail in the real world.

Web designers make this mistake frequently. They choose a typeface that works beautifully at desktop size and never check what it looks like on mobile. The result is a responsive design that looks terrible responsively.

Ignoring System Fonts

Designers often overlook system fonts: Helvetica, Arial, Georgia, San Francisco, Segoe UI. These fonts were designed specifically for screens and are pre-installed on every computer. Using them means no font loading delays, perfect rendering, and guaranteed readability.

Yet designers reject them as boring. They want "unique" typefaces. This often results in loading seven different font files, slowing the page, and introducing rendering issues. A well-chosen system font, properly sized and spaced, is often the right choice.

"The best typeface is the one that serves the message. Not the one that shows off the designer's taste."
