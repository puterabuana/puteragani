The Art of Pricing: The Pricing Models That Work

P
Putera Gani
May 13, 2026 9 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Business & Analytics

Understanding why pricing matters is the first step. The second is knowing which pricing model to use — because the structure of your pricing is as important as the number itself. A brilliant price point delivered through the wrong model will still underperform. The model determines how customers experience your pricing, how they compare you to alternatives, and how your revenue scales as your business grows.

There is no universal answer. The right model depends on your product, your customer, your competitive landscape, and your growth stage. But there are four models that have proven themselves across industries and market conditions — and understanding each one deeply is essential for any founder or business leader making serious pricing decisions.

Model 01

Value-Based Pricing

Price is set based on the perceived value to the customer, not the cost to produce. Requires deep customer research and a clear understanding of the economic outcome your product delivers.

Best for:B2B software, consulting, specialized services
Model 02

Tiered / Good-Better-Best

Multiple price points targeting different customer segments. The middle tier is typically the anchor — designed to make the premium tier look reasonable and the basic tier look limited.

Best for:SaaS products, media subscriptions, services
Model 03

Usage-Based / Consumption

Customers pay for what they use. Lowers the barrier to entry, aligns cost with value received, and scales naturally with customer growth. Requires robust metering infrastructure.

Best for:Cloud infrastructure, APIs, data platforms
Model 04

Freemium-to-Paid

A free tier acquires users at scale; a paid tier converts the most engaged. Works when the free tier creates genuine value and the paid tier offers a compelling upgrade path — not just feature removal.

Best for:Consumer apps, developer tools, productivity software

The SaaS Revolution

How Subscription Pricing Changed Software Economics

No industry has been more transformed by pricing innovation than software. For decades, software was sold as a one-time license — you paid once, you owned it forever, and the vendor had to convince you to upgrade every few years. The economics were brutal: lumpy revenue, high customer acquisition costs, and a constant pressure to ship new versions to justify the next purchase cycle.

The shift to subscription pricing — pioneered by Salesforce and then adopted by virtually every software company in existence — changed all of that. Monthly recurring revenue smoothed the financial model, aligned vendor incentives with customer success, and created a fundamentally different relationship between software companies and their users. When you charge monthly, you have to earn the renewal every single month. That pressure, counterintuitively, makes products better.

The SaaS pricing revolution also introduced a new set of metrics that have become the lingua franca of the software industry: ARR (annual recurring revenue), NRR (net revenue retention), LTV (lifetime value), and CAC (customer acquisition cost). These metrics don't just measure performance — they encode a philosophy. A company with 120% NRR is growing from its existing customer base alone, even before acquiring a single new customer. That is a fundamentally different business than one with 80% NRR, regardless of what the top-line revenue looks like.

Enterprise Pricing

Why Enterprise Software Is Priced So Differently

If you've ever looked at enterprise software pricing and been confused by the absence of actual numbers — "contact sales for pricing" — you've encountered one of the most deliberate strategies in business. Enterprise pricing is opaque by design, and for good reason.

Enterprise deals are not transactions; they are negotiations. The price is not a fixed number but a function of the customer's size, the scope of deployment, the competitive alternatives in play, the urgency of the purchase, and the relationship between the sales team and the buyer. Publishing a price would anchor the negotiation at the wrong level — either too low for large enterprises who would happily pay more, or too high for mid-market buyers who need a different package.

Enterprise pricing also reflects the true cost of enterprise sales: long sales cycles, multiple stakeholders, security reviews, legal negotiations, and ongoing customer success investment. A $100,000 annual contract that takes six months to close and requires a dedicated customer success manager is not the same as a $100,000 contract that closes in a week and runs on autopilot. The pricing has to account for the full cost of the relationship, not just the product.

💡 The Packaging Insight

How you structure your pricing matters as much as the number itself. Packaging is the art of deciding what goes in each tier, what features are gated, and what the upgrade path looks like. A poorly packaged product will underperform even at the right price point. The best packaging creates a natural progression where customers start at a lower tier, grow into it, and feel the pull toward the next level — not because features are artificially withheld, but because the higher tier genuinely serves their expanded needs.

The Freemium Trap — and How to Escape It

Freemium is seductive. It lowers acquisition costs, generates word-of-mouth, and builds a large user base quickly. But it is also one of the most commonly misused pricing models in the industry. The trap is this: a free tier that is too good eliminates the incentive to upgrade. A free tier that is too limited drives users away before they experience enough value to convert.

The companies that have made freemium work — Slack, Dropbox, Notion, Figma — share a common characteristic: their free tier is genuinely useful, but it creates natural friction at the point of growth. Slack's free tier limits message history. Dropbox's free tier limits storage. Notion's free tier limits collaboration. These limits are not arbitrary — they are precisely calibrated to the moment when a user's engagement has grown enough that the upgrade feels like a natural next step, not a punishment.

"Pricing is not a number. It's a system. The number is the output of a process that starts with understanding your customer, your value, and your market — and ends with a structure that makes the right choice obvious."

— Patrick Campbell, Founder of ProfitWell
