The Art of Pricing: How to Price Your Own Business

P
Putera Gani
May 13, 2026 9 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Entrepreneurship

Theory is useful. But at some point, you have to set an actual number. This final section is about the practical work of pricing — the research, the testing, the iteration, and the discipline required to build a pricing strategy that holds up over time. It is not a one-time exercise. It is an ongoing practice, and the businesses that treat it that way consistently outperform those that don't.

The five steps below are not a formula. They are a framework — a way of thinking about pricing that keeps you grounded in customer reality rather than internal assumptions. Work through them in order, revisit them regularly, and resist the temptation to skip the research phase in favor of the comfort of a quick decision.

🔍
Step 1: Research Willingness to Pay

Before you set any price, you need to know what your customers would actually pay. Use the Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter — ask customers at what price the product seems too cheap, a bargain, expensive, and too expensive. The overlap between "bargain" and "expensive" is your acceptable price range. This is not guesswork; it is data.

🧪
Step 2: Test Price Points

Run pricing experiments before committing. For digital products, A/B test different price points with new visitors. For services, quote different prices to different prospects and track conversion rates. You are looking for the price at which conversion drops meaningfully — that tells you where the ceiling is. Most businesses discover their ceiling is higher than they assumed.

📦
Step 3: Build Tiers Deliberately

If you offer multiple tiers, design them with a specific customer segment in mind for each. The entry tier should serve a real use case, not just be a stripped-down version of the main product. The premium tier should offer genuine additional value, not just more of the same. The middle tier should be the one you most want customers to choose — and it should be priced to make that choice feel obvious.

📣
Step 4: Communicate Value, Not Features

Your pricing page is a sales document. It should lead with outcomes, not feature lists. "Save 10 hours per week" is more compelling than "advanced automation tools." "Trusted by 5,000 growing businesses" is more persuasive than "enterprise-grade infrastructure." The price you charge is only as defensible as the value story you tell around it.

📅
Step 5: Review Pricing Quarterly

Pricing is not a set-and-forget decision. Your costs change. Your value proposition evolves. Your competitive landscape shifts. Your customer base matures. A quarterly pricing review — even a brief one — ensures that your price stays aligned with your market position. Most businesses that haven't raised prices in two years are leaving significant revenue on the table.

Common Mistakes

Pricing Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Mistake

Pricing identically to competitors — assuming that matching the market rate is safe, when it actually signals that you have no differentiated value to offer.

Fix

Identify one or two dimensions where your product genuinely outperforms alternatives — speed, reliability, support quality, integration depth — and price to reflect that advantage. If you can't identify any, that's a product problem, not a pricing problem.

Mistake

Discounting as a default sales tactic — training customers to wait for discounts, eroding margin, and signaling that your list price was never real to begin with.

Fix

Replace discounts with value additions: extended trials, onboarding support, additional seats, or implementation assistance. These cost you less than a price reduction and preserve the integrity of your pricing. Reserve discounts for strategic situations — annual prepayment, volume commitments, or specific market segments — not as a response to hesitation.

Mistake

Never raising prices on existing customers — letting loyalty become a subsidy, where your best long-term customers pay the least while new customers pay market rate.

Fix

Build price increase cadences into your customer relationships from the start. Communicate increases with advance notice, clear rationale, and genuine value additions. Customers who have experienced your product's value are far more likely to accept a reasonable price increase than you expect — especially if you've been delivering results.

Pricing Audit

Six Questions Every Business Should Answer About Their Pricing

# Question What a Good Answer Looks Like
Q1 Do you know your customers' willingness to pay? You have data from surveys, experiments, or sales conversations — not just intuition.
Q2 When did you last change your prices? Within the last 12–18 months. If it's been longer, you're almost certainly underpriced.
Q3 Do your prices reflect your current value proposition? Your pricing has evolved alongside your product. Early-stage prices don't apply to a mature product.
Q4 Are your tiers designed around customer segments? Each tier serves a distinct customer type with distinct needs — not just different feature counts.
Q5 Do you know your price-to-value ratio vs. competitors? You can articulate why your price is justified relative to alternatives — and customers agree.
Q6 Is your pricing page optimized for conversion? It leads with outcomes, uses anchoring deliberately, and makes the recommended tier obvious.
The Confidence Argument

Why Raising Your Prices Is Almost Always the Right Move

Here is the uncomfortable truth that most pricing conversations eventually arrive at: if you haven't raised your prices in the last year, you are almost certainly undercharging. Inflation alone erodes the real value of a fixed price over time. But beyond inflation, your product has almost certainly improved, your brand has grown, your customer success track record has deepened, and your competitive position has strengthened. None of that is reflected in a price that hasn't moved.

The fear of raising prices is real, but it is almost always disproportionate to the actual risk. Studies consistently show that the majority of customers who are told about a price increase accept it — particularly when the increase is communicated with advance notice, a clear rationale, and evidence of continued value delivery. The customers who leave over a price increase are typically the most price-sensitive customers — the ones with the lowest lifetime value and the highest support burden. Losing them is often a net positive.

There is also a signaling effect. A business that raises its prices is a business that believes in its own value. That confidence is visible to customers, to prospects, and to the market. It attracts a different kind of customer — one who is buying on value, not on price — and repels the kind of customer who will always be looking for a cheaper alternative. Your price is a filter. Make sure it's filtering for the customers you actually want.

💡

Pricing Is a Practice, Not a Decision

The businesses that win on pricing are not the ones that got it right once. They are the ones that built a culture of pricing discipline — researching continuously, testing regularly, communicating confidently, and iterating without apology. Price is the most powerful lever in your business model. Pull it with intention.
