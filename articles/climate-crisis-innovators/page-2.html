Pulling Carbon from the Sky: The Science of Carbon Removal

P
Putera Buana Gani
Jan 30, 2025 11 min read
Page 2 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Clean Technology

Even if humanity stopped emitting carbon tomorrow — an impossibility — the CO₂ already in the atmosphere would continue warming the planet for decades. This is the uncomfortable physics of climate: the problem has momentum. Reducing future emissions is necessary but no longer sufficient on its own.

This is why a new field of climate innovation has emerged, focused entirely on the opposite direction: not reducing emissions going forward, but actively pulling carbon that already exists in the atmosphere back out. These technologies go by several names — carbon removal, carbon dioxide removal (CDR), or negative emissions technologies. They represent one of the most ambitious engineering challenges in human history.

🌿 Nature's Lead

Trees and oceans have been performing carbon removal for billions of years. Photosynthesis captures CO₂ and locks it into organic matter. The ocean absorbs roughly 25% of all CO₂ emissions annually. Carbon removal technologies are essentially attempts to dramatically accelerate what nature already does — or to build entirely new pathways that nature never developed.

Direct Air Capture: The Carbon Vacuum

The most technologically dramatic approach is Direct Air Capture (DAC): industrial facilities that chemically extract CO₂ directly from ambient air. The concept sounds simple. The engineering is extraordinarily difficult.

CO₂ makes up just 0.04% of the atmosphere — roughly 420 parts per million. Concentrating something that dilute requires enormous amounts of energy and clever chemistry. Current DAC plants cost between $400 and $1,000 per tonne of CO₂ removed — far too expensive to deploy at meaningful scale with current technology.

Yet costs are falling. Climeworks, a Swiss company operating the world's largest DAC plant in Iceland, has announced plans to reduce costs to under $300 per tonne by 2030, and below $100 by the late 2030s. If those targets are met, DAC becomes economically viable at large scale. The race is to find the cost curve that solar found in the 2010s.

🏭 Mammoth: The World's Largest DAC Plant

Climeworks' Mammoth facility in Iceland captures up to 36,000 tonnes of CO₂ per year — tiny relative to global needs, but 10 times the capacity of its predecessor. The captured carbon is injected into basaltic rock formations, where it mineralizes into stone within two years. This is permanent geological storage — CO₂ locked away not for decades, but for millennia.

Nature-Based Solutions

Working With Nature: Forests, Soil, and Oceans

Before industrial carbon removal, there is the original technology: nature itself. Protecting and restoring forests, wetlands, and grasslands represents the most immediately available and cost-effective carbon removal pathway. Forests alone store approximately 861 billion tonnes of carbon — more than twice the amount currently in the atmosphere.

The problem is permanence. A forest planted today can be cut down or burned in a wildfire tomorrow. Climate change itself threatens the stability of forest carbon stores — warming temperatures and more frequent droughts are already turning some Amazon regions from carbon sinks into carbon sources. Nature-based solutions are essential but insufficient on their own.

🌳

Reforestation & Afforestation

Planting trees on degraded or previously cleared land. The Bonn Challenge aims to restore 350 million hectares of forests by 2030. Done well, this is among the cheapest carbon removal available at $5–50 per tonne.

🌾

Soil Carbon Sequestration

Changing agricultural practices — reduced tillage, cover cropping, compost application — can increase the carbon stored in farmland soils. Global agricultural soils could potentially store an additional 1.85 Gt of carbon per year.

🌊

Ocean-Based Removal

Approaches ranging from growing seaweed at scale, to iron fertilization of ocean waters to stimulate phytoplankton growth, to enhanced ocean alkalinity — adding crushed minerals to increase the ocean's capacity to absorb CO₂.

🔥

Bioenergy with Carbon Capture (BECCS)

Growing biomass that absorbs CO₂, burning it for energy, then capturing the resulting emissions and storing them underground. A theoretically carbon-negative energy source — if land, water, and biodiversity tradeoffs can be managed.

The Portfolio Imperative

No single carbon removal approach will be sufficient. Each has different costs, timelines, permanence levels, and co-benefits or tradeoffs. The emerging scientific consensus is that we need a diverse portfolio — nature-based solutions delivering lower-cost, immediately scalable removal, while engineered solutions like DAC are brought down the cost curve for the longer term.

🌿 Nature-Based
Lower cost, immediately available, co-benefits for biodiversity. Less permanent, vulnerable to climate impacts and land-use pressures.
🏭 Engineered
High permanence, measurable and verifiable. Currently high cost, energy-intensive, requires significant scale-up investment.

The analogy is a medical treatment portfolio. You don't choose between medication and surgery — you deploy each where it's most effective, at the right time, in the right combination. Climate solutions demand the same systems thinking.
