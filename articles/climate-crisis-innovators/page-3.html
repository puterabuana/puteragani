Fusion Energy, Geoengineering, and Whether Innovation Alone Can Save Us

P
Putera Buana Gani
Jan 30, 2025 11 min read
Page 3 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Future Energy

There is a technology that produces no carbon emissions, generates no long-lived radioactive waste, runs on the most abundant element in the universe, and could produce virtually limitless clean energy. It has been 30 years away for the last 70 years. Now, for the first time, it might actually be 10 years away.

Nuclear fusion — the same process that powers the sun — reached a historic milestone in December 2022, when scientists at the National Ignition Facility in California achieved "ignition": a fusion reaction that produced more energy than the lasers used to trigger it. It was a proof of concept 70 years in the making. The physics works. What remains is the engineering.

⚛️ What Fusion Actually Is

Conventional nuclear power splits heavy atoms apart (fission). Fusion does the opposite — it forces light atoms, typically hydrogen isotopes, together under extreme pressure and temperature, releasing enormous energy. The fuel is seawater. The byproduct is helium. There is no chain reaction that can spiral into a meltdown. If something goes wrong, the reaction simply stops.

The Private Fusion Race

What's changed in the past decade isn't just the physics — it's the economics and urgency. Climate crisis has created a trillion-dollar incentive to solve fusion. And for the first time, private capital has flooded into fusion research in a way that government programs never could.

Over 40 private fusion companies now exist worldwide, backed by billions in venture capital. Commonwealth Fusion Systems, spun out of MIT, aims to have a demonstration plant online by 2025 and commercial power by the early 2030s. TAE Technologies, Helion Energy, and others each take different technical approaches — tokamaks, field-reversed configurations, inertial confinement.

The Controversial Option

Geoengineering: The Last Resort Nobody Wants to Need

Beyond removing carbon lies an even more controversial frontier: directly intervening in Earth's climate systems to reduce warming in real time. Solar geoengineering — specifically, stratospheric aerosol injection — involves releasing reflective particles into the upper atmosphere to partially block incoming sunlight, cooling the planet directly.

The concept is based on observable reality: major volcanic eruptions that inject sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere reliably produce temporary global cooling. The 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo cooled Earth by approximately 0.5°C for two years. The question is whether we could deliberately replicate this effect — and whether we should.

⚠️ The Termination Shock Problem

The most dangerous aspect of solar geoengineering is not starting it — it's stopping it. If the world begins reflecting sunlight to mask warming, and then stops abruptly (due to political conflict, economic collapse, or any disruption), the suppressed warming would return rapidly. A "termination shock" could cause warming several times faster than the gradual change we are trying to avoid, with potentially catastrophic consequences for ecosystems and agriculture.

For this reason, most climate scientists view solar geoengineering not as a solution but as a potential emergency measure — something to be researched carefully, governed internationally, and deployed only in the most extreme scenarios. It is a last resort precisely because of what it might necessitate.

The Timeline That Matters

The honest reckoning with all of these technologies comes down to one question: how fast? The physics and engineering work. The innovations are real. The cost curves are moving in the right direction. But climate operates on a timeline that doesn't negotiate.

Now — 2030: Deploy What We Have

Massive acceleration of existing solar, wind, and energy storage. Electrification of transport. Halt of deforestation. These technologies are ready. The barrier is political will and capital deployment, not innovation.

2030 — 2040: Scale the Emerging

Green hydrogen for industry. Next-generation nuclear fission for baseload power. Direct air capture scaled to tens of millions of tonnes per year. Cost curves on these technologies must bend dramatically this decade.

2040 — 2050: Deploy the Transformative

Commercial fusion energy if development timelines hold. Gigaton-scale carbon removal. Full decarbonization of hard-to-abate sectors. Net-zero emissions globally, with active drawdown of legacy atmospheric CO₂.

The Deeper Question

Innovation Is Necessary. It's Not Sufficient.

Here is the uncomfortable truth that no technology roadmap fully captures: the climate crisis is not primarily a technology problem. The technologies we have today — solar, wind, batteries, efficiency — are already sufficient to dramatically reduce emissions if deployed at the required scale.

What has impeded deployment is not technological. It is political resistance funded by incumbent fossil fuel interests, the distribution of costs and benefits across nations and generations, the difficulty of international coordination, and the sheer institutional inertia of energy systems built over a century. Innovation can change what's possible. It cannot change what's chosen.

The innovators racing to fix the climate deserve celebration and support. Their work matters enormously. But the story of climate change will not be written in laboratories — it will be written in legislatures, boardrooms, streets, and ballot boxes. Technology is the tool. We are the ones who must choose to use it.

The climate crisis is, ultimately, a test of civilization's capacity for collective long-term action. We have the science. We are developing the tools. The remaining question — the only question that history will remember — is whether we had the will.
