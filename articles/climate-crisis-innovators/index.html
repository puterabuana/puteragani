The Climate Crisis and the Innovators Racing to Fix It

P
Putera Buana Gani
Jan 30, 2025 11 min read
Page 1 of 3
Photo: Unsplash / Climate & Environment

The numbers are no longer abstract. Average global temperatures have risen over 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels. Arctic ice sheets are retreating at record speeds. Extreme weather events — wildfires, floods, superstorms — have become the defining vocabulary of our era. The climate emergency is not a future problem. It is today's emergency, demanding today's solutions.

Yet for the first time in human history, we also possess something remarkable: an unprecedented arsenal of technologies capable of fundamentally altering our relationship with the planet. From machines that pull carbon directly from the air, to reactors that harness the same energy as the sun — the innovators racing to fix our climate are building things that were science fiction a decade ago.

🌍 The Core Challenge

To limit warming to 1.5°C, humanity must reach net-zero carbon emissions by 2050 and actively remove billions of tonnes of CO₂ already in the atmosphere. No single technology can achieve this. What's required is a portfolio — an entire civilizational transformation across energy, agriculture, transport, and industry simultaneously.

The Scale of What We're Up Against

Understanding the innovations requires first understanding the problem's scale. Humanity currently emits approximately 37 billion tonnes of CO₂ per year. Every tonne that enters the atmosphere stays there for centuries. The carbon we emitted during the Industrial Revolution is still warming the planet today.

This means emissions reductions alone — even dramatic ones — are no longer sufficient. Scientists increasingly agree that we must also actively remove carbon that already exists in the atmosphere. We must simultaneously build the clean energy infrastructure to replace fossil fuels, while also engineering solutions to reverse what the past two centuries of industrialization have done.

The Energy Transition

Solar and Wind: The Revolution Already Happening

The most consequential climate story of the past decade isn't a scientific breakthrough — it's an economic one. The cost of solar electricity has fallen by more than 90% since 2010. Wind power has dropped similarly. Renewable energy is now, in most of the world, the cheapest form of new electricity generation ever built.

This matters enormously because the core challenge of the energy transition is not technological — it is economic and political. Clean energy now wins on price. The barriers that remain are largely about grid infrastructure, storage, and the vested interests of fossil fuel incumbents — not about whether renewables work.

⚡ The Price Revolution

In 2010, solar power cost around $400 per megawatt-hour. By 2024, new solar installations in sunny regions are delivering electricity at under $20 per megawatt-hour — cheaper than operating existing coal or gas plants. The economics of clean energy have permanently shifted. The question is no longer whether we can afford to transition — it's whether we can move fast enough.

The Hardest Problems: Industry and Transport

Electricity is the easier half. The harder challenge is decarbonizing sectors that can't simply be electrified: steel production, cement manufacturing, long-haul shipping, and aviation together represent roughly one-third of all global emissions.

These are the sectors where the most interesting — and most urgent — innovation battles are being fought. Steel made with hydrogen instead of coal. Cement produced through electrochemical processes. Ships powered by ammonia. Aircraft running on sustainable aviation fuel. Each of these represents an industry being rebuilt from its molecular foundations.

🏭 The Hard-to-Abate Sectors
01

Steel Production

Traditional steelmaking uses coal as both fuel and chemical agent. Green hydrogen can replace coal, producing water as a byproduct instead of CO₂. Sweden's HYBRIT project has already produced the world's first fossil-free steel.

02

Cement & Concrete

Concrete production accounts for 8% of global CO₂. Novel cements, carbon-cured concrete, and electrochemical processes are all being developed to eliminate these emissions without sacrificing material performance.

03

Aviation

Sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) made from biological or synthetic sources can reduce lifecycle emissions by up to 80%. Electric aircraft for short routes and hydrogen-powered planes for longer routes are advancing rapidly.

04

Shipping

Maritime shipping carries 90% of world trade. Ammonia, methanol, and hydrogen are all being trialled as zero-carbon marine fuels. Maersk has already ordered ammonia-capable vessels for delivery in the late 2020s.

What's remarkable is that for each of these apparently intractable problems, viable technical pathways now exist. The challenge has shifted from "can we?" to "how fast can we scale?" — and that is a fundamentally different, and more tractable, kind of problem.
